What it is

Most people have never heard of the Congressional Award. That's partly by design. It isn't handed out at a ceremony you get invited to. There's no national ranking, no competition to enter. You either put in the work over years and earn it, or you don't.

Congress established the award in 1979 to recognize young Americans who set personal goals and achieve them through sustained effort. It covers four activity areas, and at the Gold Medal level, you have to demonstrate real commitment across all four: not just checking boxes, but logging meaningful hours and documenting what you actually did.

It is attainable, but it was harder than I expected. Balancing the time it requires with school and everything else you do can be challenging. Keeping track of everything: the hours, the dates, the months, and how it all adds up. The final push to hit the next level of volunteer public service hours while studying for the ACT and working part time. Now that I've hit the requirements, I still have to go through a review process with possible revisions. Around 800 people earned the Gold Medal last year. I started pursuing mine on September 13, 2023, the beginning of my 9th grade year.

Why this matters

Most people reading this won't know what the Congressional Award is. That's fine. The point isn't the credential. It's what it required me to actually do over nearly three years, and what it says about how I operate.

I didn't approach the service hours as a checklist. I went back to the same organizations year after year because I genuinely like working with kids. I didn't treat the expedition as a formality. I planned it around my thesis, built a real route, and spent five days in conversations that a classroom couldn't replicate.

It's a multi-year pattern of showing up. Not a single impressive weekend.
